Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Valuation of Volkswagen AG

Using several approaches, Volkswagen AG (VW) is valued on January 01, 2024. Weighted Cost of Capital (WACC) approach is performed with an assumption of constant debt ratio over time. Using Adjusted Present Value (APV) with a constant debt amount over time, the result is deviated from the market cap. By adjusting the debt amount over time, the estimated equity value intersected with the market cap of VW. Further, sensitivity analysis is studied on WACC approach and pointed sensitive parameters of the company. Option to wait one year is studied with empirical free cash flows (FCF). Also, Merton model is applied to find the adjusted debt ratio and credit spread with accounting default risk.

The valuation of Volkswagen Group through discounted cash flow (DCF) methods, including Adjusted Present Value (APV) and Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC), is detailed in this study. It encompasses a three-year short-term forecast and a medium-term outlook, utilizing financial data from the past decade. The APV approach considers Free Cash Flow (FCF) and tax shields discounted separately, resulting in a total company value of €164.05 billion, despite a discrepancy in total equity due to predetermined book values of debt. Conversely, WACC-based valuation yields a total value of €145.316 billion, with an alternative WACC accounting for default probability resulting in a slightly lower valuation. Comparative analysis reveals surprising outcomes, where APV valuation exceeds WACC valuation due to substantial total debt relative to present company value, indicating the need for debt ratio adjustments. Sensitivity analyses explore the impact of parameters such as debt ratio, tax rates, and growth rates on firm value, revealing significant fluctuations. Real option analysis suggests that waiting for VW yields a superior strategy, as it aligns with the firm's current market capitalization. Notably, the study sheds light on Volkswagen's challenges post the NOx emission scandal and applies Merton Model for further financial analysis. Additionally, it provides insights into the company's tax rates and sensitivity to various parameters, essential for comprehensive financial modeling and decision-making.

Proximity Penalty of COVID-19 "Outbreak" on Stock Markets

The COVID-19 pandemic had a significant impact on global stock markets, particularly during the early stages before its official declaration as a pandemic by the World Health Organization (WHO). As the outbreak rapidly spread, the potential for spillover effects became a substantial risk for financial markets. This paper investigates the effect of COVID-19 on stock market returns, specifically focusing on the geographical proximity to the epicenter of the outbreak, Wuhan. By examining the relationship between distance to Wuhan and stock market returns, this study aims to shed light on the influence of geographical proximity on the financial implications of the pandemic.

Valuation of Express Certificate on Royal Dutch Shell Company

The valuation of an express certificate on Royal Dutch Shell Company involved Monte Carlo Simulation and Portfolio Insurance strategies. Using Geometric Brownian Motion with 65,536 observations, probable pricing paths of the underlying were simulated, resulting in an estimated certificate price of €112.49. Figures depicted the estimated prices of the certificate, historic prices of the underlying stock, and the comparison between actual and estimated prices. Sensitivity and stress scenario analyses were conducted to measure the effectiveness of put option fractions in portfolios, revealing significant average estimated returns under different conditions. Risk management strategies, such as including put options in portfolios, were explored to manage tail risk. Additionally, the Nelson-Siegel-Svennson method was employed to estimate the risk-free rate, and various derivative pricing methods were applied to analyze delta, gamma, and vega. The study provided insights into product characteristics, early termination scenarios, and risk management practices in the context of financial derivatives.

Empirical Asset Pricing Assignment 3

The study presents three different approaches to applying the GMM in order to estimate the two parameters (β and γ) of the Consumption-Based Asset Pricing Equation (BAPE), which employs a power utility function and gross returns. The three approaches that aim to solve the minimization problem of the Objective-Based GMM are:

  1. Performing a grid search that relies on a fixed set of values for β and γ.
  2. First-stage GMM.
  3. Second-stage GMM.

Next is an assessment of how well each method fits the data, including computing the correlation between the Stochastic Discount Factor (SDF) of each method and the consumption growth. Different approaches include plotting model-implied mean returns against the market returns and conducting hypothesis testing for the most conventional values for β = 1 and γ = 10, and commenting on the spread range of the confidence interval, especially for γ.

AKIS - Deep Learning Transcription Tool for Ottoman Writings

We propose an automatic recognition system for printed Ottoman documents which transcribes Ottoman texts directly to the modern Turkish script. We evaluated three decoding strategies including the Word Beam Search decoder that allows to use a recognition lexicon and n-gram statistics during the decoding phase. The system achieves 2.25% character error rate and 6.42% word error rate on a test set of 1.4K samples, using the test set transcriptions as the recogni- tion lexicon. Using a general purpose, large lexicon of the Ottoman era (260K words and 77% test coverage), the performance is measured as 3.68% character error rate and 16.61% word error rate.
